6  Level 5: Hyperbolic PDEs ‚Äî Wave Equation

Pavni: Acharya, we‚Äôve studied the heat equation. From theory we know that it seems to spread information everywhere. Does every PDE behave like that?

Acharya: That‚Äôs a good observation, Pavni. Not every PDE diffuses information infinitely fast like the parabolic heat equation.
Some equations describe situations where information or disturbances travel at a finite speed ‚Äî like waves or moving traffic.

Pavni: So these are called hyperbolic equations?

Acharya: Exactly. Hyperbolic equations are the mathematical way of describing signals or quantities that move through a medium rather than diffuse.
For example ‚Äî a vibrating string, sound waves in air, or even cars moving on a road.

Pavni: Interesting! But why are they so different from diffusion equations like the heat equation?

Acharya: Because diffusion equations allow disturbances to spread instantaneously everywhere ‚Äî a small change at one point affects all others immediately.
That‚Äôs called infinite propagation speed.
But hyperbolic equations restrict influence to certain paths in the \((x,t)\)-plane, called characteristics.
Information travels only along these paths at a finite speed.

Pavni: That sounds more physical. After all, signals, sound, or cars don‚Äôt move infinitely fast!

Acharya: Exactly. Let‚Äôs begin with a simple and familiar example ‚Äî traffic flow.
It beautifully captures the idea of conservation and finite-speed motion.

6.1 üöó The Traffic Flow Model

Pavni: So, where do we begin?

Acharya: Let‚Äôs define some quantities.

  • \(\rho(x,t)\): density of cars at position \(x\) and time \(t\) (cars/km)
  • \(v(x,t)\): average speed of cars (km/h)
  • \(q(x,t)\): flow rate ‚Äî number of cars passing a point per unit time (cars/hour)

Pavni: So \(q\) must depend on both how many cars there are and how fast they move?

Acharya: Precisely. That leads us to the simple physical relation: \[ q = \rho v. \]

üí° Why \(q = \rho v\)? If a small segment of road of length \(\Delta x\) contains \(\rho \, \Delta x\) cars,
and each car moves with velocity \(v\), then in time \(\Delta t\) each car covers \(v \, \Delta t\) km.
So, the number of cars passing a fixed point in time \(\Delta t\) is
\[ (\rho \, \Delta x) \cdot \frac{v \, \Delta t}{\Delta x} = \rho v \, \Delta t. \] Dividing by \(\Delta t\) gives \(q = \rho v\).

6.2 ‚öôÔ∏è Conservation of Cars

Acharya: Now, think of a small stretch of road between \(x_1\) and \(x_2\).
Cars can only leave or enter through the ends.

\[ \frac{d}{dt}\int_{x_1}^{x_2} \rho(x,t)\,dx = q(x_1,t) - q(x_2,t). \]

Pavni: That looks like conservation of mass!

Acharya: Exactly ‚Äî just conservation of the number of cars.
Simplifying, we get the conservation equation: \[ \rho_t + q_x = 0. \]

6.3 üõ£Ô∏è Constant-Speed Motion: The Linear Advection Equation

Acharya: Suppose every car moves with a constant speed \(a > 0\). Then \(v(x,t) = a\).
Hence, \(q = \rho v = a\rho\).

Substitute into the conservation law: \[ \rho_t + (a\rho)_x = 0 \quad \Rightarrow \quad \rho_t + a\rho_x = 0. \]

Pavni: That‚Äôs our PDE?

Acharya: Yes, it‚Äôs called the linear advection equation.
It‚Äôs the simplest hyperbolic equation ‚Äî it models how a quantity moves steadily with constant speed.

Pavni: So this describes a traffic pattern moving forward without changing its shape?

Acharya: Exactly. The entire profile of car density just shifts rightward with speed \(a\).

6.4 ‚úèÔ∏è Try to Predict

Acharya: Before solving it, can you guess how the density will evolve if initially the density is a bump ‚Äî say, \[ \rho(x,0) = e^{-x^2}? \]

Pavni: I think it will move to the right, keeping its shape.

Acharya: Perfect intuition. We‚Äôll now confirm this mathematically using characteristics ‚Äî special curves along which information travels.

6.5 üåä Characteristics and the Solution of the Linear Advection Equation

Pavni: Acharya, now that we have derived the equation
\[ \rho_t + a\rho_x = 0, \quad a > 0, \]
for cars moving at constant speed, how do we actually find \(\rho(x,t)\) from this?

Acharya: Excellent question. This equation may look simple, but it contains a beautiful idea ‚Äî that information travels along certain paths in the \((x,t)\)-plane. These paths are called characteristics.

6.5.1 1Ô∏è‚É£ Deriving the Characteristic Curves

Acharya: Let‚Äôs think of \(\rho\) as a function of both \(x\) and \(t\).
Suppose we move along a curve \(x = x(t)\) in the \((x,t)\)-plane.
By the chain rule, the total derivative of \(\rho\) along that curve is

\[ \frac{d\rho}{dt} = \rho_t + \frac{dx}{dt}\rho_x. \]

Now, if we choose \(\dfrac{dx}{dt} = a\), then

\[ \frac{d\rho}{dt} = \rho_t + a\rho_x = 0. \]

That means \(\rho\) is constant along any curve that satisfies \(\dfrac{dx}{dt} = a\).

Pavni: So those are the characteristic curves?

Acharya: Exactly! Integrating \(\dfrac{dx}{dt} = a\) gives

\[ x = a t + x_0, \quad \text{or} \quad x - a t = x_0 = \text{constant}. \]

Each line \(x - a t = \text{constant}\) is a characteristic line.

6.5.2 2Ô∏è‚É£ The Meaning of Characteristics

Pavni: What does it mean that \(\rho\) is constant along these lines?

Acharya: It means that the value of \(\rho\) at time \(t\) and position \(x\)
is exactly the same as its value at the point where that characteristic line started on the \(x\)-axis (that is, at time \(t=0\)).

So, \[ \rho(x,t) = \rho_0(x - a t), \] where \(\rho_0(x)\) is the initial density at \(t=0\).

Pavni: Oh! So the initial profile just shifts by \(a t\)?

Acharya: Exactly ‚Äî it moves to the right if \(a>0\) and to the left if \(a<0\).
No change in shape ‚Äî pure translation.

6.5.3 3Ô∏è‚É£ Verifying the Solution

Pavni: Let‚Äôs check if this really satisfies the PDE.

Acharya: Sure! Let \(\rho(x,t) = \rho_0(x - a t)\).
Then \[ \rho_t = -a\rho_0'(x - a t), \quad \rho_x = \rho_0'(x - a t). \] Substitute into \(\rho_t + a\rho_x = 0\): \[ (-a\rho_0') + a\rho_0' = 0. \] ‚úÖ It satisfies the equation perfectly.

6.5.4 4Ô∏è‚É£ Understanding Finite Propagation Speed

Acharya: The key property of this equation is that information moves at a finite speed.

If a disturbance is initially present only between \(x=0\) and \(x=1\),
then at time \(t\) it will be found only between \(x=a t\) and \(x=1+a t\).
Every point outside this region remains unaffected.

Pavni: So information doesn‚Äôt spread instantly like in the heat equation?

Acharya: Exactly. In the heat equation, even a small bump affects the whole line immediately ‚Äî infinite propagation speed.
But here, the influence travels only along straight lines \(x - a t = \text{constant}\) ‚Äî that‚Äôs finite-speed propagation.

6.5.5 5Ô∏è‚É£ Example

Let‚Äôs take an initial density \[ \rho_0(x) = \begin{cases} 1, & 0 < x < 1, \\ 0, & \text{otherwise}. \end{cases} \]

Then \[ \rho(x,t) = \begin{cases} 1, & a t < x < 1 + a t, \\ 0, & \text{otherwise}. \end{cases} \]

Pavni: So the block of cars just moves forward as a group?

Acharya: Exactly ‚Äî like a moving traffic wave with constant shape.

# Level 5: Hyperbolic PDEs ‚Äî Wave Equation

**Pavni:** Acharya, we‚Äôve studied the heat equation. From theory we know that it seems to *spread* information everywhere. Does every PDE behave like that?

**Acharya:** That‚Äôs a good observation, Pavni. Not every PDE diffuses information infinitely fast like the parabolic heat equation.  
Some equations describe situations where information or disturbances travel at a *finite speed* ‚Äî like waves or moving traffic.

**Pavni:** So these are called *hyperbolic equations*?

**Acharya:** Exactly. Hyperbolic equations are the mathematical way of describing signals or quantities that *move* through a medium rather than *diffuse*.  
For example ‚Äî a vibrating string, sound waves in air, or even cars moving on a road.

**Pavni:** Interesting! But why are they so different from diffusion equations like the heat equation?

**Acharya:** Because diffusion equations allow disturbances to spread instantaneously everywhere ‚Äî a small change at one point affects all others immediately.  
That‚Äôs called **infinite propagation speed**.  
But hyperbolic equations restrict influence to certain *paths* in the $(x,t)$-plane, called **characteristics**.  
Information travels only along these paths at a finite speed.

**Pavni:** That sounds more physical. After all, signals, sound, or cars don‚Äôt move infinitely fast!

**Acharya:** Exactly. Let‚Äôs begin with a simple and familiar example ‚Äî **traffic flow**.  
It beautifully captures the idea of *conservation* and *finite-speed motion*.

---

## üöó The Traffic Flow Model

**Pavni:** So, where do we begin?

**Acharya:** Let‚Äôs define some quantities.

- $\rho(x,t)$: density of cars at position $x$ and time $t$ (cars/km)  
- $v(x,t)$: average speed of cars (km/h)  
- $q(x,t)$: flow rate ‚Äî number of cars passing a point per unit time (cars/hour)

**Pavni:** So $q$ must depend on both how many cars there are and how fast they move?

**Acharya:** Precisely. That leads us to the simple physical relation:
$$
q = \rho v.
$$

<details>
<summary>üí° Why $q = \rho v$?</summary>

If a small segment of road of length $\Delta x$ contains $\rho \, \Delta x$ cars,  
and each car moves with velocity $v$, then in time $\Delta t$ each car covers $v \, \Delta t$ km.  
So, the number of cars passing a fixed point in time $\Delta t$ is  
$$
(\rho \, \Delta x) \cdot \frac{v \, \Delta t}{\Delta x} = \rho v \, \Delta t.
$$
Dividing by $\Delta t$ gives $q = \rho v$.
</details>

---

## ‚öôÔ∏è Conservation of Cars

**Acharya:** Now, think of a small stretch of road between $x_1$ and $x_2$.  
Cars can only leave or enter through the ends.

$$
\frac{d}{dt}\int_{x_1}^{x_2} \rho(x,t)\,dx = q(x_1,t) - q(x_2,t).
$$

**Pavni:** That looks like conservation of mass!

**Acharya:** Exactly ‚Äî just conservation of the *number of cars*.  
Simplifying, we get the **conservation equation**:
$$
\rho_t + q_x = 0.
$$

---

## üõ£Ô∏è Constant-Speed Motion: The Linear Advection Equation

**Acharya:** Suppose every car moves with a **constant speed** $a > 0$. Then $v(x,t) = a$.  
Hence, $q = \rho v = a\rho$.

Substitute into the conservation law:
$$
\rho_t + (a\rho)_x = 0 \quad \Rightarrow \quad \rho_t + a\rho_x = 0.
$$

**Pavni:** That‚Äôs our PDE?

**Acharya:** Yes, it‚Äôs called the **linear advection equation**.  
It‚Äôs the simplest hyperbolic equation ‚Äî it models how a quantity moves steadily with constant speed.

**Pavni:** So this describes a traffic pattern moving forward without changing its shape?

**Acharya:** Exactly. The entire profile of car density just shifts rightward with speed $a$.

---

## ‚úèÔ∏è Try to Predict

**Acharya:** Before solving it, can you guess how the density will evolve if initially the density is a bump ‚Äî say,
$$
\rho(x,0) = e^{-x^2}?
$$

**Pavni:** I think it will move to the right, keeping its shape.

**Acharya:** Perfect intuition. We‚Äôll now confirm this mathematically using **characteristics** ‚Äî special curves along which information travels.

## üåä Characteristics and the Solution of the Linear Advection Equation

**Pavni:** Acharya, now that we have derived the equation  
$$
\rho_t + a\rho_x = 0, \quad a > 0,
$$  
for cars moving at constant speed, how do we actually find $\rho(x,t)$ from this?

**Acharya:** Excellent question. This equation may look simple, but it contains a beautiful idea ‚Äî that **information travels along certain paths** in the $(x,t)$-plane. These paths are called *characteristics*.

---

### 1Ô∏è‚É£ Deriving the Characteristic Curves

**Acharya:** Let‚Äôs think of $\rho$ as a function of both $x$ and $t$.  
Suppose we move along a curve $x = x(t)$ in the $(x,t)$-plane.  
By the chain rule, the total derivative of $\rho$ along that curve is

$$
\frac{d\rho}{dt} = \rho_t + \frac{dx}{dt}\rho_x.
$$

Now, if we choose $\dfrac{dx}{dt} = a$, then

$$
\frac{d\rho}{dt} = \rho_t + a\rho_x = 0.
$$

That means $\rho$ is **constant** along any curve that satisfies $\dfrac{dx}{dt} = a$.

**Pavni:** So those are the characteristic curves?

**Acharya:** Exactly! Integrating $\dfrac{dx}{dt} = a$ gives

$$
x = a t + x_0, \quad \text{or} \quad x - a t = x_0 = \text{constant}.
$$

Each line $x - a t = \text{constant}$ is a **characteristic line**.

---

### 2Ô∏è‚É£ The Meaning of Characteristics

**Pavni:** What does it mean that $\rho$ is constant along these lines?

**Acharya:** It means that the value of $\rho$ at time $t$ and position $x$  
is exactly the same as its value at the point where that characteristic line started on the $x$-axis (that is, at time $t=0$).

So,
$$
\rho(x,t) = \rho_0(x - a t),
$$
where $\rho_0(x)$ is the initial density at $t=0$.

**Pavni:** Oh! So the initial profile just shifts by $a t$?

**Acharya:** Exactly ‚Äî it moves to the right if $a>0$ and to the left if $a<0$.  
No change in shape ‚Äî pure translation.

---

### 3Ô∏è‚É£ Verifying the Solution

**Pavni:** Let‚Äôs check if this really satisfies the PDE.

**Acharya:** Sure! Let $\rho(x,t) = \rho_0(x - a t)$.  
Then
$$
\rho_t = -a\rho_0'(x - a t), \quad \rho_x = \rho_0'(x - a t).
$$
Substitute into $\rho_t + a\rho_x = 0$:
$$
(-a\rho_0') + a\rho_0' = 0.
$$
‚úÖ It satisfies the equation perfectly.

---

### 4Ô∏è‚É£ Understanding Finite Propagation Speed

**Acharya:** The key property of this equation is that information moves at a *finite speed*.

If a disturbance is initially present only between $x=0$ and $x=1$,  
then at time $t$ it will be found only between $x=a t$ and $x=1+a t$.  
Every point outside this region remains unaffected.

**Pavni:** So information doesn‚Äôt spread instantly like in the heat equation?

**Acharya:** Exactly. In the **heat equation**, even a small bump affects the whole line immediately ‚Äî infinite propagation speed.  
But here, the influence travels only along straight lines $x - a t = \text{constant}$ ‚Äî that‚Äôs **finite-speed propagation**.

---

### 5Ô∏è‚É£ Example

Let‚Äôs take an initial density
$$
\rho_0(x) =
\begin{cases}
1, & 0 < x < 1, \\
0, & \text{otherwise}.
\end{cases}
$$

Then
$$
\rho(x,t) =
\begin{cases}
1, & a t < x < 1 + a t, \\
0, & \text{otherwise}.
\end{cases}
$$

**Pavni:** So the block of cars just moves forward as a group?

**Acharya:** Exactly ‚Äî like a moving traffic wave with constant shape.
